There's a useful test for whether an AI application is genuinely valuable or just impressive-sounding: ask who couldn't be served without it.

For most AI tools, the honest answer is that the people being served could have been served another way — maybe less efficiently, maybe at higher cost, but still served. The customer would have gotten a response eventually. The document would have been summarized by a human. The code would have been debugged at some point.

For the International Rescue Committee's growing portfolio of AI tools, the answer to that question is different. The refugee navigating USCIS paperwork at 2am when no caseworker is available might have no one else to ask. The child in Borno State, Nigeria — where Boko Haram destroyed hundreds of schools over fifteen years of conflict — might have no classroom to attend. The trafficking survivor connected with a first responder who doesn't know what to look for might slip through undetected. In each of those cases, the counterfactual isn't "less efficiently served." It's "not served at all."

That distinction matters. The IRC's approach to AI is worth examining not because it's technically the most sophisticated, but because it's applying AI where the gap between "with" and "without" is measured in human lives.

## ALMA: A Lifeline for Newcomers

On November 10, 2025, the IRC launched ALMA — a multilingual virtual assistant designed specifically for refugees, Special Immigrant Visa holders, and other newcomers arriving in the United States.

The US immigration and resettlement system is genuinely hard to navigate. The forms, the deadlines, the agency acronyms, the rights and responsibilities — it's complex for a native English speaker. For someone arriving from Afghanistan or South Sudan or Myanmar, often traumatized, often without formal education in their native language, often deeply unfamiliar with American bureaucratic culture, it's a labyrinth.

IRC caseworkers do extraordinary work helping newcomers navigate this. But a caseworker has a caseload, has office hours, has a language capacity. ALMA doesn't. It operates in multiple languages, around the clock, handling the kinds of procedural questions that consume enormous amounts of caseworker time: How do I renew my work authorization? What documents do I need for my appointment? What is my case status? Where is the nearest legal aid clinic?

The goal isn't to replace the human relationship between a caseworker and a client. It's to free up that relationship for the things only a human can do — the trust-building, the trauma-informed support, the navigation of complex situations that require judgment and nuance. ALMA handles the procedural layer so the human layer can go deeper.

What's noteworthy about the design is the IRC's explicit commitment to responsible AI in a high-stakes context. When you're building for people in vulnerable situations — people who may distrust institutions, who may have experienced harm from automated systems, who have very little margin for error — the ethics bar is higher than it is for a productivity tool. The IRC has been public about the guardrails and oversight structures they've built in.

## aprendIA: School Where There Is No School

Northeast Nigeria — specifically Borno, Adamawa, and Yobe States — has been the epicenter of the Boko Haram insurgency for more than 15 years. The group's name roughly translates to "Western education is forbidden." They have made good on that mission: thousands of schools were attacked, burned, or abandoned during the conflict. Teachers fled. Children grew up in displacement camps where education was sporadic at best.

The IRC has been working in this region for years. AprendIA is their AI-supported educational chatbot platform that operates through mobile messaging apps — which means it works on the cheap, often-prepaid smartphones that displaced populations actually have, at any hour, without requiring physical school infrastructure.

The scale of adoption tells you something important about the need. The program started with a controlled pilot of around 400 teachers. By early 2026, it had reached 3,200 teachers in the IRC's Northeast Nigeria program. The target before the end of 2026 is a user base of over 22,000 — and a goal of 1 million learners globally by the same date.

Teachers are often the right first target for tools like this. A teacher who understands how to use AI-assisted curriculum delivery can reach dozens or hundreds of children. Equipping teachers — especially in contexts where their own formal training may have been disrupted by conflict — multiplies impact in a way that direct-to-child platforms can't always achieve.

Think about what it means for a child in Borno State to have access to structured learning content through a phone when there's no school building, no reliable electricity, and the nearest trained teacher might be in a different town. AprendIA doesn't solve the conflict that created this situation. But it refuses to let the situation become a permanent educational gap.

## AntiTraffickingResponse.org: AI as a First Responder Tool

In October 2025, the IRC partnered with Hewlett Packard Enterprise to launch AntiTraffickingResponse.org — a digital platform using AI to help first responders identify trafficking situations and guide survivors toward safe employment and recovery resources.

Human trafficking is notoriously difficult to detect. It happens in plain sight — in restaurants, nail salons, agricultural fields, domestic households. First responders — police officers, emergency room nurses, social workers, hotel staff — are often the people most likely to encounter trafficking situations, but training alone doesn't give them the pattern recognition they need. A trafficking victim may present as a willing participant. They may be coached by their trafficker to give specific answers. The signs are subtle and context-dependent.

The IRC's platform equips these frontline workers with AI-supported guidance: questions to ask, behaviors to look for, local resources to connect survivors with. It's not replacing human judgment — it's augmenting it with pattern recognition and resource knowledge that training sessions can only partially provide.

This matters more in 2026 than it did five years ago. Human trafficking has expanded alongside both global migration crises and the digital economy, with recruiters increasingly operating across borders and through online platforms. The survivors who reach the IRC are often the ones who encountered a first responder who knew what to look for. The rest remain invisible.

## The Signpost AI Platform: Infrastructure for the Field

Underneath all of these applications is something the IRC is building that may matter even more long-term: Signpost AI, an agent-building platform developed in partnership with Google.org, Zendesk, and Cisco. It's designed to let humanitarian organizations create, test, and deploy AI agents with purpose-built safety guardrails for high-stakes contexts.

This is infrastructure thinking. Instead of the IRC building every AI application from scratch — or worse, each humanitarian organization building its own disconnected tools — Signpost AI provides a shared foundation with safety and ethics baked in at the platform level.

For the humanitarian sector, which is perpetually under-resourced and cannot afford the AI research and development budgets of commercial organizations, this kind of shared infrastructure is a multiplier. An NGO focused on flood response in Bangladesh shouldn't need to figure out from scratch how to build a responsible AI chatbot. It should be able to reach for a platform that the IRC and its partners have already made safe and adaptable.

## What This Actually Represents

I think the IRC's AI work is worth highlighting not because any individual tool is technically groundbreaking, but because of the organizational posture it represents.

The humanitarian sector is under enormous financial pressure in 2026. USAID has been gutted. Major donors are cutting back. The UN is operating with a $23 billion funding gap for humanitarian response. In this environment, organizations face a choice: do more with less, or serve fewer people. AI, deployed thoughtfully, is one of the few levers that can expand reach without expanding cost.

But "thoughtfully" is doing a lot of work in that sentence. When your users are among the most vulnerable people on Earth — people who have survived conflict, displacement, trafficking, and poverty — the cost of a poorly designed AI system isn't a bad product review. It's harm.

The IRC's approach — building responsibly, with explicit ethical frameworks, with human oversight, with a recognition that automation is a means not an end — is the model for what this kind of work should look like.

That's not a small thing to demonstrate. In a sector that will increasingly need to turn to AI to maintain its reach, having organizations model responsible deployment is exactly the kind of precedent that shapes what comes next.
