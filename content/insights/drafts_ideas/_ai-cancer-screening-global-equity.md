If you want to understand the injustice embedded in global cancer care, start with a single number.

The 5-year survival rate for breast cancer caught at Stage 1 is approximately **99%**. At Stage 4 — when the cancer has metastasized to distant organs — that number drops to **27%**.

The disease is, in a real sense, the same. What changes everything is when it's found.

In the United States, where mammography screening programs are widespread and radiology capacity is high, the majority of breast cancer cases are caught at early stages. The 5-year survival rate averages around 91% across all stages. In sub-Saharan Africa, where screening infrastructure is sparse and radiologists are scarce, diagnosis most commonly happens at later stages, often when symptoms have become unmistakable and treatment options are more limited. The 5-year survival rate in many African countries is closer to 46%.

That is not a gap in medicine. It is a gap in access to timely information.

AI is beginning to address that gap. Not completely. Not without complications. But in ways that deserve our attention.

## What the Clinical Evidence Shows

Three developments in 2024 and 2025 help frame where AI breast cancer screening actually is:

**The MASAI trial.** In one of the largest randomized controlled trials of AI-supported mammography screening, researchers in Sweden evaluated AI as a reading assistant for radiologists. The finding: AI-supported screening was safe and diagnostically accurate — a viable clinical alternative, particularly valuable in health systems where the radiologist workforce is limited. AI didn't replace radiologist judgment; it extended it, reducing the burden on each reader while maintaining detection rates.

**The Germany study.** A large-scale prospective study across 12 sites involving **463,094 women** found that AI-supported screening increased breast cancer detection rates meaningfully without increasing the recall rate (the rate at which women are called back for additional imaging, which is a proxy for false positives). This is the key trade-off in screening — you want to find more cancers without generating more unnecessary anxiety and follow-up procedures. The Germany results suggest AI can improve detection without worsening that trade-off.

**Thermalytix in India.** This is the one I find most interesting for the global equity question.

India faces an acute radiology capacity crisis. With around 12,000 radiologists serving a population of 1.4 billion, the ratio of radiologists to population is roughly 0.9 per 100,000 — compared to 12 or more in high-income countries. Mammography machines require significant capital, dedicated facilities, regular maintenance, trained operators, and trained radiologists to interpret results. They are simply not accessible to most of India's rural population.

Thermalytix takes a fundamentally different approach. Instead of X-ray mammography, it uses thermal imaging — detecting patterns of heat emission from the breast, which differ in areas of abnormal tissue growth. A state-wide study in Punjab screened **15,069 women across 183 locations** over 18 months. Twenty-seven breast cancer cases were confirmed, yielding a detection rate of 0.18%.

Thermal imaging is not new. What's new is using AI to interpret those thermal images with diagnostic accuracy. Thermalytix provides risk stratification — categorizing women into five risk levels — without radiation, without the capital cost of mammography infrastructure, and in settings that community health workers can operate. It's the difference between needing a hospital radiology department and needing a trained field worker with a device.

## The Radiology Workforce Crisis Nobody Talks About

The WHO estimates that **70% of countries** have insufficient diagnostic imaging capacity. This isn't just a developing-country problem — it affects rural communities in wealthy countries, regions experiencing radiologist shortages, and health systems dealing with high and growing screening demand for multiple cancer types simultaneously.

The radiologist shortage has a structural cause: training a radiologist takes a decade. You can't close a 70% global deficit by training more radiologists in time to matter for people who need screening today.

This is precisely the problem AI is structurally well-suited to address. An AI model that can read a mammogram — or in Thermalytix's case, a thermal image — doesn't require a radiology residency. It can be deployed at scale, rapidly, in settings where human expertise is thin. It can work at 3am. It can cover geographies that a radiologist would never realistically serve.

Used this way, AI in cancer screening isn't replacing radiologists. It's substituting for the absence of radiologists.

## The Equity Trap: A Problem We Cannot Ignore

Here is where I want to hold two things at once, because one of them complicates the optimism.

Most of the AI systems being developed for cancer screening are trained on data from high-income countries. The MASAI trial data came from Swedish women. The large mammography AI studies draw from European and North American healthcare databases. The models learn what cancer looks like in populations that happen to be predominantly white, predominantly well-nourished, with access to high-quality imaging equipment using consistent protocols.

A study published in 2025 on global disparities in AI mammography found that **no datasets from low-income countries** have been used in training the leading AI mammography models. This is a significant problem.

Cancer presents differently across populations. Hormonal profiles differ. Disease subtypes vary. The quality and calibration of imaging equipment in low-resource settings differs from what the AI was trained on. A model that performs with 94% accuracy on a well-calibrated European mammography dataset may perform significantly worse on images from a clinic in Nigeria or Vietnam.

This is not a reason to abandon AI in cancer screening. It's a reason to be intentional — and honest — about which populations benefit from which tools, and to invest in building training datasets that represent the people the tools are meant to serve.

Thermalytix's value proposition sidesteps part of this problem by using a different modality — thermal imaging — that was developed and validated specifically in low-resource Indian settings. That's the design approach that gets this right: build for the context you're deploying in, not for the best-resourced version of the problem.

## What Needs to Happen Next

The clinical evidence is now strong that AI can meaningfully improve cancer detection. The hard work ahead is in deployment — in getting these tools to the people who need them most.

A few things need to happen simultaneously:

**Equity-aware data collection.** Research institutions and AI developers need to prioritize building training datasets from low- and middle-income country clinical settings. This is expensive and logistically complex, but without it, the AI systems being deployed globally will systematically underserve the populations with the highest unmet need.

**Community health worker integration.** The Thermalytix model points in the right direction: tools that community health workers can operate, in the field, without needing radiologists. Scaling this approach requires investment in training, device distribution, and the data infrastructure to route flagged cases to clinical follow-up.

**Health system pathways.** Early detection is only valuable if there's a pathway to treatment. In many low-income settings, identifying a cancer is the beginning of a different crisis — accessing surgery, radiation, or chemotherapy. AI screening programs that don't account for what happens after a positive result can create cruel outcomes: awareness of a disease with no ability to address it.

**Policy and funding alignment.** The tools exist. The clinical evidence is accumulating. What's missing is the policy will and funding to deploy them at the scale and in the geographies where they matter most. This requires governments, international health organizations, and philanthropic funders to treat AI-assisted screening as a public health priority, not a technology curiosity.

## Why This Moment Matters

Breast cancer is not the only cancer where early detection dramatically improves survival. Cervical cancer, colorectal cancer, lung cancer — across many of the leading causes of cancer death globally, the survival curve tracks stage at diagnosis in ways that make early detection a stronger determinant of outcome than treatment advances alone.

AI that makes screening faster, cheaper, and more accessible doesn't just help the people it directly screens. It changes the population-level dynamics of how many cancers get caught early versus late. At scale, across a country, that shift in the distribution of stage-at-diagnosis can translate into thousands of lives saved annually.

The 99% and the 27% are not fixed numbers. They reflect the current distribution of access to early detection — and that distribution is something we have the technical capacity to change.

The question, as always, is whether we have the will to design and fund the deployment in a way that actually reaches the people for whom the gap is widest.
